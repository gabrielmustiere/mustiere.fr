Technical decisions have a strange property: their upfront cost is almost always underestimated by a factor of 2, and their long-term cost almost always underestimated by a factor of 10.

## The visible price and the hidden one

When you pick a database, a framework, an auth pattern, you look at:

- Implementation time.
- License fees and infrastructure cost.
- The learning curve for the team.

These are tangible numbers, easy to defend in a meeting. What rarely gets looked at:

- The **per-feature tax** the decision will levy. Over 3 years, every feature pays 5, 10, sometimes 30% of its cost as tribute to that structural decision.
- The exit cost the day you want to change. For some decisions (data schema, authz), that cost approaches infinity.

## How to estimate the tax

Empirically, from ten structural decisions I've watched age:

- A bad data abstraction imposes a tax of **15 to 40%** on every feature that touches that data.
- A poorly-matched framework costs **5 to 15%** per feature, mostly from debugging friction.
- A rushed security/authz decision costs upwards of **20%** as soon as you start managing multiple roles.

These ranges aren't universal truths. But asking the team — "how much heavier does this decision make every future PR?" — produces surprisingly convergent estimates.

## The 15% rule

My rule: if the estimated tax is above 15% per feature and the debt can't be paid off in a sprint, refactor within six months. Below that, live with it — it's cheaper than the surgery.

The most expensive part isn't the initial decision. It's dragging it along for three years, hoping you'll find the time, someday, to fix it.
