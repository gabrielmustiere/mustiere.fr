## What I've watched transform over fourteen years

I've been working in PHP/Symfony since 2012. It's a deliberate specialisation: stacking project after project in the same ecosystem builds an intuition that
makes every new scoping faster, every delivery more predictable, every maintenance simpler. It isn't an opportunistic choice — it's what lets me, on most of
the projects I take on, ship in days what would warrant weeks elsewhere.

Over those fourteen years, the ecosystem went through several transformations that deserve to be looked at together rather than separately:

- The language moved from a permissive playground to a typed, strict and now fast language. PHP 8.5 has very little in common with the PHP 5.3 of my early
  career.
- Symfony established itself as the foundation, then spawned a complete ecosystem — API Platform, Sylius, UX Initiative, Mercure — that covers most needs
  without having to glue pieces together across projects.
- Tooling has become unavoidable. PHPStan, Rector, PHP-CS-Fixer, PHPUnit, Composer, Symfony CLI…: quality no longer relies on individual discipline, it's
  carried by tools cleanly integrated into CI.
- Infrastructure took a late but striking leap with FrankenPHP in worker mode. Performance, long pitched as the language's structural weakness, no longer is
  one.
- More recently, initiatives like Symfony UX, Symfony AI or the Hotwire Native bridge extend the ecosystem into territory where it was less expected: a
  reactive front end, AI-first apps and mobile.

The sections that follow take these movements one by one, with what I actually use in production. The language, the frameworks, the tooling, the
infrastructure, front and mobile, AI. At the end, a five-criteria grid to place where the PHP ecosystem is particularly relevant.
