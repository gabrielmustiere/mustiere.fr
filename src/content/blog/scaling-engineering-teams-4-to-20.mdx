There's a cliché floating around: "scaling a tech team means hiring." It's wrong. Hiring is the visible part. The real difficulty is that each threshold forces you to **unlearn** what worked at the previous stage.

## The three breaking points

I've walked half a dozen teams through this trajectory. The same ruptures show up every time — at roughly the same sizes, regardless of domain.

### 4 → 8: the end of "everyone knows"

At 4, everyone knows what everyone else is doing. No need to document, formally prioritise, or structure code reviews. By 5 or 6 people, that model cracks. One person's commits surprise another. Decisions get made at the coffee machine, and whoever wasn't there discovers them in production.

What needs to be in place at this threshold:
- A single place where technical decisions are written down (a `docs/decisions/` folder with short ADRs does the job).
- A review cadence, even a minimal one.
- A channel where anyone can ask any technical question without friction.

### 8 → 12: the end of shared ownership

At 8, anyone can still touch anything. At 12, that stops working — not for technical reasons, for cognitive ones. No one holds the full context in their head anymore.

This is the moment to carve out clear *areas of responsibility*. Not airtight silos: zones where one or two people are accountable. The rest of the team can still contribute, but final arbitration belongs to the owner.

### 12 → 20: the end of the single tech lead

At 12, one lead can still hold coherence together. At 20, they become the bottleneck. Every cross-cutting decision routes through them, they stop coding, and the team slows to their pace.

The fix isn't to name more leads — it's to **give up on perfect coherence**. Accept that some choices will differ across domains, and that cross-team review should hold to minimal standards (security, data), not style.

## What gets underestimated almost every time

Async communication demands more rigour than sync, not less. Standups become useless before weekly meetings do. Recruiting eats 40% of senior engineers' time and nobody values it the way they should.

And above all: every threshold adds a month of organisational drift before the team finds its rhythm again. Plan for it.
